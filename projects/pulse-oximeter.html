Arduino-Based Pulse Oximeter

This university project focused on building an Arduino-based pulse oximeter prototype for measuring heart rate and estimating blood oxygen saturation from optical signals transmitted through a fingertip. The device used red, infrared and white LEDs together with a phototransistor. Heart rate was determined from the periodic variation in transmitted light caused by pulsatile blood flow, while oxygen saturation was estimated from the relationship between the red and infrared optical signals.

Project Overview

Pulse oximetry is based on the different absorption characteristics of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. In this project, light from red and infrared LEDs was transmitted through a fingertip and measured using a phototransistor.

The periodic variation in the optical signal was used to estimate heart rate. Blood oxygen saturation was approximated from the ratio between the pulsatile components of the red and infrared measurements.

A white LED was also included to reduce the relative influence of ambient light. The measured values were processed using an Arduino Uno and displayed in real time on a 16 × 2 LCD screen.

Project Work

  • Assembled the measurement circuit as part of a three-person project team.
  • Connected the red, infrared and white LEDs and the phototransistor to an Arduino Uno.
  • Selected and installed the required circuit resistors.
  • Configured the I2C address of the LCD display.
  • Modified the calibration parameters used for oxygen-saturation estimation.
  • Recorded red and infrared optical signals from a fingertip.
  • Compared the prototype with a commercial pulse oximeter.
  • Analysed measurement accuracy and practical limitations.

Measurement Principle

  1. Illuminate the fingertip using red and infrared LEDs.
  2. Measure the transmitted light using a phototransistor.
  3. Identify periodic optical variations caused by blood flow.
  4. Estimate heart rate from the time between successive pulse cycles.
  5. Calculate the ratio between the red and infrared signal variations.
  6. Convert the optical ratio into an approximate oxygen-saturation value.
  7. Display the results on the LCD screen.

Results

The prototype was able to detect pulsatile changes in the red and infrared optical signals and provide estimates of heart rate and blood oxygen saturation.

Measurements were compared with a commercial pulse oximeter during normal breathing and breath-holding tests. The prototype produced indicative oxygen-saturation values, but the results were not sufficiently accurate for medical use.

Key Learning Outcomes

The project provided practical experience in optical physiological measurement, Arduino-based hardware integration and sensor calibration. It also demonstrated how motion, ambient light, component tolerances and limited calibration data can affect the reliability of biomedical measurements.
