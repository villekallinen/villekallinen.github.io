Parameter Estimation on an Inclined Plane

This experimental data-analysis project investigated the estimation of physical parameters from motion measurements collected on an inclined plane.

The moments of inertia of two cylindrical objects and the kinetic friction coefficient of a sliding rectangular object were estimated using linear, nonlinear and weighted least-squares methods.

Project Overview

The objective of the project was to estimate mechanical parameters that could not be measured directly. Cylindrical objects were allowed to roll down an inclined plane, while a rectangular object was allowed to slide along the same surface.

Two optical gates connected to an oscilloscope were used to measure the travel time between known positions. Measurements were repeated at several inclination angles to create datasets suitable for parameter estimation.

Mathematical observation models were derived from Newton's equations of translational and rotational motion. The unknown parameters were then estimated by fitting these models to the experimental measurements.

Analysis Workflow

  1. Measure the object dimensions, masses and geometry of the experimental setup.
  2. Set the inclined plane to a known angle.
  3. Measure the travel time between two optical gates.
  4. Repeat each measurement several times.
  5. Calculate the mean travel time and its measurement uncertainty.
  6. Construct nonlinear observation models from the equations of motion.
  7. Estimate the unknown parameters using nonlinear least squares.
  8. Apply weighted least squares using the measurement uncertainties.
  9. Linearise the rolling-object model and perform linear estimation.
  10. Compare the experimental estimates with theoretical parameter values.

Experimental Setup

Two cylindrical objects and one rectangular object were measured on an adjustable inclined plane. The cylinders were measured at ten inclination angles between 3 and 30 degrees. The sliding object required larger angles because static friction prevented motion at the smallest inclinations.

Six repeated measurements were performed for each object and inclination angle. The measured travel time was defined as the time required for the front edge of the object to move from the first optical gate to the second.

Results

The moments of inertia of both cylindrical objects and the kinetic friction coefficient of the sliding object were successfully estimated from the experimental measurements.

Comparing ordinary, weighted and linearised estimation methods demonstrated how observation-model selection and measurement uncertainty influence the final parameter estimates.
